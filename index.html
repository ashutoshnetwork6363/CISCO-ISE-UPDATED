Platform Overview

The Foundation of Zero Trust Security

Cisco Identity Services Engine (ISE) is an enterprise-class **Network Access Control (NAC)** and identity management platform. Think of it as a highly intelligent digital security guard for corporate network infrastructure.

In a modern Zero Trust architecture, you cannot assume a device or user is safe just because they are plugged into a physical switch port or connected to office Wi-Fi. Cisco ISE acts as the centralized gatekeeper, validating **who** is connecting, **what** device they are using, and **where** they are trying to go before allowing entry.

Essential Concepts You Must Know First

Before navigating the Cisco ISE GUI dashboard, you must master the fundamental network behaviors that govern network access control.

What is a NAD?

A Network Access Device (NAD) is any hardware switch, wireless LAN controller (WLC), or VPN gateway that sits between the endpoint client device and the internal corporate network.

*Key Note: ISE does not connect physically to users. Users connect to a NAD, and the NAD asks ISE what to do with them.

RADIUS vs. TACACS+

ISE uses two distinct UDP/TCP protocol channels to listen to network traffic requests:

  • RADIUS (UDP 1812/1813): Used for Network Access. Validates regular users and devices connecting via ports/Wi-Fi.
  • TACACS+ (TCP 49): Used for Device Administration. Secures logins for network administrators commands on switches and routers.

802.1X vs. MAB Access

When an endpoint attaches to a switch port monitored by ISE, access is processed using one of two methods:

  • 802.1X: The golden standard. The device actively runs a software supplicant that securely transmits a username/password or a hardware digital certificate.
  • MAB (MAC Authentication Bypass): Used for simple, legacy devices (like printers). The NAD captures the device's MAC address and passes it to ISE to verify against a whitelist.

Phase 2: First-Time GUI Login & Trust Infrastructure

Once the CLI shows the Application Server is fully up, you can open your web browser and navigate to your configured IP via HTTPS: https://<your_ise_ip_address>/admin. [cite: 58] Log in using the same admin credentials you configured during the console setup. [cite: 59, 60]

Upon the glorious first login, you will be prompted to accept the EULA and navigate past the initial smart licensing banners. [cite: 61] Out of the box, ISE automatically activates a 90-day Evaluation License, allowing you to test out all functionalities (such as Premier, Advantage, and Device Admin/TACACS+) with a limit of 100 endpoints before you need to connect it to your Cisco Smart Account. [cite: 62, 63]

From here, your very next logical tasks are to install your trusted CA Certificates and integrate the box into your Active Directory or external identity store. [cite: 64] Now that the appliance is up and running, you can log into the web GUI. [cite: 65] However, before you start adding network devices or creating authentication rules, you must establish the identity and trust infrastructure. [cite: 66] Without these next steps, the ISE box cannot securely communicate with your network, your endpoints, or your administrative accounts. [cite: 67] Here is the exact sequence to move from a freshly booted box to a production-ready node: [cite: 68]

1. Join Active Directory

Since you are managing a large-scale enterprise environment, your users and machine accounts likely live in Active Directory (AD). [cite: 70] Joining ISE to the domain is the absolute first configuration task. [cite: 71]

  • Administration: In the ISE GUI, go to the menu and choose Active Directory (Identity Management > External Identity Sources). [cite: 72, 73, 74] Click Add, enter your Active Directory Domain Name (e.g., corp.local), and give the connection a name. [cite: 75] Click Submit. [cite: 76]
  • Provide Join Credentials: ISE will ask if you want to join all nodes to the domain. Click Join. [cite: 77, 78] You will be prompted to enter the username and password of an AD account with permissions to add computer objects to the domain. [cite: 79]
  • Select Relevant AD Groups: Once the status changes to Operational, click on the Groups tab. [cite: 80, 81] Click Select Groups from AD, search for, and check the boxes for the groups you will use in your policies (e.g., Domain Computers, Domain Users, Network_Admins). [cite: 82] Click Save. [cite: 83]

2. Generate and Install Certificates

Out of the box, ISE uses self-signed certificates. This causes browser warning errors for admins and, more importantly, will cause 802.1X authentications to fail because endpoints will not trust a self-signed certificate. [cite: 85] You need to replace these with certificates signed by your Internal Enterprise Certificate Authority (CA). [cite: 86]

  • Step A (Generate CSR): Go to Administration > System > Certificates > Certificate Signing Requests. [cite: 87, 88] Click Generate Certificate Signing Requests (CSR). [cite: 89] Choose the usage (Select Multi-Use to cover both EAP authentication and Admin GUI access). [cite: 90] Select your ISE node, fill out the Subject fields (Common Name = your ISE FQDN), and ensure you add the FQDN and IP address to the Subject Alternative Name (SAN) fields. [cite: 91] Click Generate and then Export the CSR text file. [cite: 92]
  • Step B (Submit & Bind): Take that CSR text, paste it into your internal CA web enrollment tool, and download the issued certificate in .cer or .p7b format. [cite: 93, 94] Also, download your CA's Root/Certificate Chain. [cite: 95] Back in ISE, go to Trusted Certificates and click Import. Upload your CA's Root and Intermediate certificates first so ISE trusts the issuer. [cite: 96, 97] Go back to Certificate Signing Requests, check the box next to your pending CSR, and click Bind Certificate. [cite: 98] Upload the issued certificate file, give it a friendly name, and check the boxes to assign it for Admin (GUI access) and EAP Authentication (for dot1x). [cite: 99]

🔄 Note: Saving this will cause the ISE application services to restart automatically to apply the new certificate. It will take another 10–15 minutes to come back online. [cite: 100, 101]

3. Define the Network Device Groups & NADs

Once the box comes back up with its trusted certificate and AD connection, you must tell ISE which Network Access Devices (NADs)—your switches, wireless controllers, and VPN gateways—are allowed to talk to it. [cite: 102, 103]

Step Action Why It Matters
1. Create Groups [cite: 104] Go to Administration > Network Resources > Network Device Groups. Organize by Location or Device Type (e.g., Switches_Access, Firewalls_VPN). [cite: 104] Simplifies policy writing later. You can apply one rule to an entire group of devices instead of individual IPs. [cite: 104]
2. Add the NAD [cite: 104] Go to Network Devices and click Add. Enter the Name, IP address (or subnet), and assign it to the groups you made. [cite: 104] Tells ISE to listen for RADIUS/TACACS requests coming from this specific source IP. [cite: 104]
3. Set Shared Secrets [cite: 104] Scroll down to RADIUS Settings / TACACS Settings, check the box, and enter a secure Shared Secret. [cite: 104] This password must match exactly what you configure on your switch or firewall CLI (radius-server key ). [cite: 104]

4. Map GUI Administrators to Active Directory

As a best practice, you should never share the local CLI/GUI admin account created during setup. [cite: 106] Now that AD is linked, map your team's network admin group to ISE GUI administration. [cite: 107]

Go to Administration > System > Admin Access > Administrators > Admin Groups. [cite: 108] Click Add and create an external group mapped to your AD Network_Admins group. [cite: 109] Go to Admin Access > Authorization > Permissions (or Policy) and assign this external group the Super Admin menu access privilege. [cite: 110] Now your foundational prep work is complete. The ISE box is secure, integrated into your corporate network identity, and ready for you to build out your actual Authentication and Authorization policies. [cite: 111]

Phase 3: Core Engine Setup & Policies

Now that your ISE box is securely tied to Active Directory, trusts your internal Certificate Authority, and knows about your Network Access Devices (NADs), you are ready for the core engine setup: building your first actual policies. [cite: 112] At this stage, you want to set up the logical progression that handles incoming connection requests. [cite: 113] In ISE, this means configuring Allowed Protocols, defining Policy Elements (Results), and finally constructing your Authentication and Authorization Policies. [cite: 114, 115]

1. Configure the Allowed Protocols [cite: 116]

Before writing rules, you need to tell ISE how it is allowed to negotiate security protocols with endpoints. [cite: 117] Navigate to Policy > Policy Elements > Results > Authentication > Allowed Protocols. [cite: 118] Click Add to create a custom service, or inspect the Default Network Access profile. [cite: 119] For enterprise security (like machine/user authentications), ensure PEAP (Protected Extensible Authentication Protocol) and EAP-TLS (if using client certificates) are enabled. [cite: 120] For security, uncheck insecure protocols like PAP/ASCII or LEAP unless an old legacy device explicitly requires them. Click Save. [cite: 121, 122]

2. Define Your Authorization Profiles (The "What You Get" Phase) [cite: 123]

An Authorization Profile dictates the network payload or permission configuration that ISE pushes down to a switch port or wireless controller after a user successfully authenticates. [cite: 124] Go to Policy > Policy Elements > Results > Authorization > Authorization Profiles and click Add. [cite: 125, 126, 127]

  • Create a Compliant Profile (e.g., Employee Access): [cite: 128] Name the profile (e.g., Employee_Access_Profile). Under Common Tasks, check the VLAN box and type your corporate VLAN ID or name (e.g., 10 or VLAN_Employees). You can also specify a downloadable ACL (dACL) if filtering traffic at the port level. [cite: 129, 130]
  • Create a Quarantine/Non-Compliant Profile: [cite: 131] Create a second profile named Quarantine_Profile. Map this to a restricted guest or remediation VLAN. This is where you will dump devices that fail authentication or profiling checks. [cite: 132, 133]

3. Construct the Authentication Policy [cite: 134]

Now we build the actual decision matrix under Policy > Policy Sets. [cite: 135] If you are using the default policy set, click the > arrow on the right to view its inner rules. [cite: 136] First, you hit the Authentication Policy section. This determines who you are checking and where to look. [cite: 137]

Authentication Rule Strategy: [cite: 139]
IF [ Wired_802.1X ] THEN USE [ Active_Directory ] [cite: 140]
  • Create a Rule: Click the + icon to add an authentication rule. [cite: 141]
  • Conditions: Choose a pre-defined condition like Wired_802.1X or Wireless_802.1X (ISE evaluates the incoming RADIUS attributes to match this). [cite: 142]
  • Use Identity Store: Change this from Internal Users to the Active Directory instance you joined in the previous step. [cite: 143] This tells ISE: "If someone plugs into a dot1x port, check their credentials against our Windows domain controller." [cite: 144]

4. Construct the Authorization Policy [cite: 145]

Directly beneath the authentication rules is the Authorization Policy. This determines what permissions the authenticated entity actually receives. [cite: 146] It evaluates from top to bottom, stopping at the first rule matched. [cite: 147] You want to build a tiered policy structure that maps your Active Directory groups to the Authorization Profiles you built in Step 2. [cite: 148]

Rule Name Condition (Who matches) Profile/Result (What they get)
Domain_Admins_Rule [cite: 149] ExternalGroups EQUALS corp.local/Network_Admins [cite: 149] PermitAccess (or full admin access) [cite: 149]
Corporate_Laptops [cite: 149] ExternalGroups EQUALS corp.local/Domain Computers [cite: 149] Employee_Access_Profile (VLAN 10) [cite: 149]
Default [cite: 149] Any [cite: 149] DenyAccess (Blocks everything else by default) [cite: 149]

💡 Pro-Tip for Large Deployments: Always keep the final Default fallback rule set to DenyAccess. [cite: 150] This enforces a zero-trust model out-of-the-box, ensuring any device that isn't explicitly accounted for in your higher rules gets shut down at the port. [cite: 151]

5. Put a Test Port in "Monitor Mode" [cite: 152]

Do not flip your entire infrastructure to enforcing mode yet! To verify everything works without breaking production traffic: Configure a single test switch port with standard 802.1X syntax. [cite: 153, 154]

Ensure you apply the command authentication open on that specific switch interface. [cite: 155] This puts the port in Monitor Mode—the switch will send RADIUS requests to ISE, but it won't actually block the traffic if it fails. [cite: 156]

Plug in a test machine, and immediately open the ISE RADIUS Live Logs (Operations > RADIUS > Live Logs). [cite: 157] Watch the real-time lines turn Green (Success) or Red (Failure) to tweak your rules before a wider deployment. [cite: 158]

Phase 4: High Availability & Maintenance

Now that your core policies are built and you are monitoring authentication requests in your test environment, the final operational phase of a new deployment shifts focus to high availability, visibility, and system maintenance. [cite: 159] You have a functioning single box, but to make it a resilient enterprise deployment, you need to execute these final foundational steps: [cite: 160]

1. Backup Repository Setup [cite: 161]

Before doing anything else, you must protect your hard work. ISE cannot run backups to its own local hard drive; it requires an external storage location called a Repository. [cite: 162, 163]

  • Define Repository: Go to Administration > System > Maintenance > Repository and click Add. Configure a secure remote destination using SFTP or NFS (e.g., Protocol: SFTP, Server/IP, Path). [cite: 164-170]
  • Schedule Backups: Navigate to Administration > System > Maintenance > Backup & Restore and set up a recurring schedule. [cite: 171, 172]
  • You need two types: Operational Backup (Configuration) contains your configuration, AD join info, and policy sets. (Run daily/weekly). [cite: 173, 174] M&T Backup (Operational Data) contains RADIUS logs, reporting data, and session histories. (Run weekly/monthly). [cite: 175, 176]

2. Secondary Node for HA [cite: 177]

In a deployment supporting thousands of users, a single ISE box represents a single point of failure. If you have a second ISE appliance, you must join it to the first to create a Deployment Cluster. [cite: 178, 179]

  • Assign Personas: Make the second box the Secondary PAN and Secondary MNT. [cite: 189, 190] Click Save. The sync process takes 20-30 minutes. [cite: 191, 192]

3. System Logging and Alerts [cite: 193]

Hook ISE into your proactive monitoring infrastructure. [cite: 194]

4. Enforcement Mode [cite: 202]

Once you have verified in the RADIUS Live Logs that your production devices are hitting the correct rules and turning Green, you are ready to transition your network switches out of test mode. [cite: 203] On your access layer switches, remove the safety net configuration from the interfaces: [cite: 204]

no authentication open [cite: 207]

With authentication open removed, the switch port will now actively drop traffic and block unauthorized users, transitioning your environment into a true Zero-Trust Secure Network Access posture. [cite: 208]

Phase 5: SD-Access & Catalyst Center TACACS+ Integration

Now we are shifting gears from basic ISE platform setup into Cisco Software-Defined Access (SD-Access) / Campus Fabric territory. [cite: 209] When you say you want a Super Admin with all access in a Fabric Site, you are talking about setting up RBAC (Role-Based Access Control) for the orchestrator controlling that fabric site—which is Cisco Catalyst Center (formerly Cisco DNA Center)—and utilizing ISE as the central identity engine via TACACS+ (Device Administration). [cite: 210]

Step 1: Enable TACACS+ and Build Profile in ISE [cite: 212]

By default, ISE only handles RADIUS. You must ensure the Device Administration Service is checked under Administration > System > Deployment. [cite: 213, 214, 215, 216] Then, for Catalyst Center to know a user is a "Super Admin," ISE must pass back a specific string attribute: ROLE=SUPER-ADMIN. [cite: 217, 218, 219]

Go to Work Centers > Device Administration > Policy Elements > Results > TACACS Outputs and click Add. [cite: 220, 221, 222] Name the Shell Profile (e.g., Catalyst_Center_Super_Admin) with Default Privilege 15. [cite: 223, 224] Under Custom Attributes, add Mandatory Attribute cisco-av-pair with Value ROLE=SUPER-ADMIN and Save. [cite: 225-230]

Step 2: Add Catalyst Center as TACACS Client & Policy [cite: 231, 238]

In ISE (Administration > Network Resources > Network Devices), add the Catalyst Center IP, check TACACS Authentication Settings, and enter a Shared Secret. [cite: 232-237]

Next, under Work Centers > Device Administration > Device Admin Policy Sets, create a set matching the Catalyst Center IP. [cite: 239-242] Inside the set, point the Authentication Policy to Active Directory. [cite: 243, 244, 245] In Authorization, map your AD Admin group to the Catalyst_Center_Super_Admin shell profile. [cite: 246-249]

Step 3: Point Catalyst Center to ISE & Verify [cite: 250, 289]

Log into Catalyst Center using the local fallback admin account (https://<catalyst_center_ip>). [cite: 251, 252, 264-271] Navigate to System > Settings > Authentication Server, add a TACACS+ server with your ISE IP and exact Shared Secret. [cite: 253, 254, 255, 256, 272-281]

Scroll down to Role Mapping, Add Role Mapping: Server Attribute SUPER-ADMIN mapped to Catalyst Center Role SUPER-ADMIN. [cite: 282-288]

Finally, open an Incognito browser, navigate to Catalyst Center, and log in with your corporate AD credentials. [cite: 290-298] You should have full Super Admin visibility, and ISE Operations > TACACS > Live Logs will show a green success line. [cite: 299-302]

Phase 6: SD-Access Macro & Micro-Segmentation

In a Cisco SD-Access Campus Fabric, we no longer manage access using traditional, static IP subnets or complex access-lists (ACLs) applied to every single switch port. [cite: 303] Instead, we use a two-tier policy model controlled centrally by Cisco Catalyst Center and enforced by Cisco ISE: [cite: 304]

  • Macro-Segmentation (Virtual Networks): Equivalent to VRFs. Devices in separate VNs cannot talk to each other unless traffic passes through an external firewall. [cite: 305]
  • Micro-Segmentation (SGTs): Happens inside a Virtual Network. We assign users to Scalable Group Tags (SGTs) like "Finance-Users" and write matrix rules. [cite: 306, 307]

Fabric Site Scenario & Blueprint [cite: 308, 309, 315]

Imagine a Corporate HQ with Finance (needs isolation), HR (standard access), and Security CCTV (IoT isolation). [cite: 310-314]

Click Deploy in Catalyst Center. It instantly communicates with ISE, compiling matrix rules into SGACLs on the fabric switches, dropping unauthorized packets instantly at the edge without traditional IP access-lists! [cite: 367-372]

Phase 7: Advanced Security Orchestration

Beyond standard 802.1X authentication, guest portals, and basic access control, Cisco ISE serves as a massive security orchestration engine. [cite: 373] Here is what else you can do when you unleash the full power of your ISE boxes: [cite: 374, 375]

1. Device Profiling [cite: 376]

ISE uses Profiling to figure out exactly what a device is without any user intervention. [cite: 377, 378] It acts like a network detective using DHCP Probes (OUI matching), HTTP Probes (user-agents), CDP/LLDP Probes, and Nmap Scans. [cite: 379-383] You can flag anomalies instantly if a device mimics a printer but uses non-printer ports. [cite: 384]

2. Endpoint Posture Assessment [cite: 385]

Posture Assessment checks the health of the endpoint before granting access using Cisco Secure Client. [cite: 386-389] It verifies BitLocker, Corporate Antivirus, and more. [cite: 390-402] If failed, ISE can automatically trigger Remediation to update definitions while holding the machine in a quarantine sandbox. [cite: 403]

3. Rapid Threat Containment (RTC) [cite: 404]

Through Adaptive Network Control (ANC) and integration with Firewalls/SIEM, ISE dynamically quarantines compromised machines. [cite: 405, 406] If a Firewall detects malware, it alerts ISE via API. ISE instantly pushes a RADIUS Change of Authorization (CoA) to the switch port, dropping the user into an isolated black-hole VLAN. [cite: 407-411]

4. BYOD & TACACS+ Auditing [cite: 412, 419]

ISE automates personal device onboarding via a Self-Service BYOD Portal, pushing certs for seamless EAP-TLS. [cite: 413-418] Additionally, for TACACS+, ISE enforces highly specific Command Sets (e.g., Level 1 Engineers can 'show', but not 'configure terminal') and maintains centralized Audit Trails of every keystroke for compliance. [cite: 420-424]

Phase 8: Deep Troubleshooting with TCP Dumps

When troubleshooting complex authentication issues—like a sticky 802.1X timeout, a misbehaving RADIUS client, or a failing Active Directory join—sometimes high-level logs aren't enough. You need to see the raw packets moving across the wire. [cite: 425, 426] Cisco ISE has a powerful built-in TCP Dump tool right in the GUI. [cite: 427, 428]

1. Extracting the Dump (GUI) [cite: 429]

  • Navigate to Operations > Troubleshoot > Diagnostic Tools > General Tools > TCP Dump. [cite: 430-435]
  • Select Host Name, Interface (e.g., eth0), and Filter (e.g., port 1812, port 49, or host 10.10.40.55). [cite: 436-442]
  • Click Start, reproduce the failure, click Stop, and download the .pcap file. [cite: 443-449]

2. Wireshark Filters [cite: 450]

  • radius - All RADIUS traffic [cite: 454]
  • tacacs - Device administration [cite: 455]
  • eap - 802.1X handshakes [cite: 456]
  • radius.code == 1 - Access-Requests only [cite: 457]
  • radius.code == 3 - Access-Rejects only [cite: 458]

3. Correlating Packets with Live Logs [cite: 459, 460]

Scenario A: "Timeout" Error [cite: 462]

Wireshark: Multiple Access-Requests, no replies. Live Logs: Nothing shows up. [cite: 463, 464]
Diagnosis: A firewall is blocking UDP ports 1812/1813, or there is a RADIUS Shared Secret mismatch. [cite: 465, 466]
